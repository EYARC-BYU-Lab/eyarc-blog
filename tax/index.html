Scenario One

In 2023, Mark, a software engineer, decided to establish Zuckerbook, a social media platform. To finance his company, he chose to crowdsource through Venmo, raising $100,000 in funds. Currently, the company operates as a sole proprietorship. The company was initially intended to be a partnership, but the tax structure fell through due to his business partner’s abusive behavior, thus the company is operating as a sole proprietorship. In the same year, Mark filed and won a lawsuit against his former partner, resulting in a $90,000 settlement for bullying, physical abuse, and physical harassment. If the $190,000 represents Mark’s total income for the year, how much of it is taxable and why?

Scenario Three

Tony, an entrepreneur, created a company called Stark Industries (SKI). He later sells the company for a profit. The following year, Tony bought 100 shares of SKI (at the time of purchase, they were worth $20 a share) to still feel like he’s a part of “his” company. Five years later, Tony’s questioning the value of SKI and so on June 1st 20X5, Tony sells all 100 shares for $15 each. On June 16th 20X5, Tony has second thoughts and decides to buy back 100 shares of SKI for $17 each. Tony is not a dealer in securities. What are the tax consequences for Tony in 20X5?

Scenario Four

In 2021, Stitch Ohana Inc. (SOI), a C Corporation, donated a total of $3,000,000 of cash to the Lilo Aloha Foundation (LAF). SOI’s taxable income before the charitable contribution deduction, the net operating loss (NOL) carryover, and the dividends received deduction (DRD) was $7,500,000. The company also had an NOL carryover of $30,000 and DRD of $20,000. What is SOI’s charitable contribution deduction for 2021? What is its charitable contribution carryover for 2022, if any? Do you believe this tax treatment is logical and fair? Why or why not?
